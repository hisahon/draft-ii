Example 1 – The 2024 Noto Earthquake (Japan)

[Maletckii & Astafyeva, 2024]

On 1 January 2024 at 07:10:09 UTC, a major M7.5 earthquake struck the Noto peninsula of Japan. Focal mechanism solutions for the earthquake indicate faulting occurred on a moderately dipping reverse fault striking to the southwest or northeast ( https://earthquake.usgs.gov/earthquakes/eventpage/us6000m0xl/executive ). The earthquake occurred during local evening pre-sunset time, which means that TIDs driven by the passage of the solar terminator are very likely to occur. Indeed, this is what we observed to the north-east from the epicenter before the earthquake in the dTEC/dt data.

The earthquake generated a disturbance in the ionosphere that is visible as quasi-periodic fluctuations in the dTEC/dt parameter. The CSID propagated from the epicentral area in the south-eastward segment, and can be clearly seen for only ~200 km of horizontal distance from the epicenter. We note that the “background” TIDs near the solar terminator have similar waveforms, and the Noto case is a good example to demonstrate the importance of distinguishing the origin of the disturbances that we detect.

Supporting Figure 1. (a) Timeline for the automatic NRT analysis of ionospheric disturbances generated by the 2024 Noto earthquake: sound speed profile (blue) and average acoustic wave velocity to reach hmF2 estimated from IRI-2016 (red) and JJ433 ionosonde (green). (b) First instantaneous velocities obtained using the D1-GNSS-RT method in NRT. (c) NRT clustering of TIDs generated by the Noto earthquake (purple) in the distance–velocity space. The timeline at the bottom shows the timing of the main steps of TID detection and characterization. The first tsunami warning by JMA was issued ~12 min after the earthquake.

We applied methods developed by Maletckii & Astafyeva (2021; 2022; 2023; 2024) to the 2024 Noto case (30-sec data). On that day, and during the time of the earthquake, the acoustic wave would take about 8.5 minutes to reach the height of the ionospheric maximum (HmF2).

In 9.5–10 minutes after the earthquake onset time, we obtain the first instantaneous velocity field, showing the direction of CSID propagation as well as their velocities. One can see that the velocity vectors show the correct direction of propagation, as seen in the demo video.

Further, in 12–15 minutes after the earthquake onset, our clustering algorithm places the Noto CSID (pink oval in panel (c)) next to previous earthquakes, indicating that the observed disturbance is of seismic origin.

Example 2 – The 2014 Iquique Earthquake (Chile)

[Brissaud & Astafyeva, 2022]

Below is an example of the automatic detection of co-seismic ionospheric disturbances due to the 2014 Iquique earthquake (Chile). It only takes about 100 seconds to capture the CSID and to estimate their arrival times. Further, one can see that this allows imaging of the seismic source area (panel (b)).

Supporting Figure 2. Ionospheric maps for the 2014 Iquique earthquake. (a) Map showing the epicentre location (yellow star) and surface projection of the fault slip (in m) as green to yellow patches. (b) CID detections using an RF-based classifier and time picker, and (c) association classes determined from confirmed detections. CID coordinates were calculated at the intersection point between the LOS and the ionospheric layer using Hion = 250 km. These maps can be generated ~15 minutes after the event.

Example 3 – The 2011 Tohoku Tsunami Arrival to the US Coast

[Maletckii & Astafyeva, 2024]

On 11 March 2011 at 05:46 UT, a M9.0 earthquake generated a large tsunami. After 11–12 hours of propagation across the Pacific Ocean, the tsunami reached the US west coast. The tsunami caused quasi-periodic disturbances in the ionosphere (panel (a)). The current version of AIDE does not detect such disturbances, but the dTEC/dt approach detected them and estimated their velocities and direction of propagation.

Panel (b) shows the instantaneous velocity fields calculated from co-tsunamic ionospheric disturbances detected by GPS satellite PRN 22. The grey arrow corresponds to 0.1 km/s. Panel (c) shows the localization of ionospheric centers estimated from the velocity vectors (blue dots). Panel (d) presents the distribution of co-tsunamic ionospheric disturbances obtained using the D1-GNSS-RT method, where the x-axis is the velocity (m/s) and the y-axis is the fraction of these velocities in the results.

Supporting Figure 3. (a) NOAA NGDC map of tsunami arrival time (hours) during the 2011 Japan tsunami (NOAA hazard map). (b) GNSS receiver network (yellow dots) used for the tsunami produced by the Tohoku earthquake on the US west coast. (c) Instantaneous velocity fields derived from co-tsunamic ionospheric disturbances detected by GPS satellite PRN 22. (d) Localization of ionospheric centres estimated from the velocity vectors (blue dots).
